This year, I entered my second decade of living, and with growing up and finding your sense of identity often comes many self-doubting crises. What should I study? Will I find a job? Do I like my friends? Where do I want to live when I’m older? Finding identity and sense of self isn’t easy and something that often comes with time; and stress about a career is often normal when we start to consider internships and working in the real world. As we know, there are many applications that use AI to help tailor resumes, apply to job positions, etc. So, I started wondering, if I gave an LLM a brief explanation of my interests, or a “walk me thorugh your resume,” would it be able to help me figure out what I want to do?

Hello! This is an excellent set of interests and experiences, and I want to start by saying that your “dilemma” is actually a tremendous strength. The ability to bridge the qualitative, critical thinking of the social sciences with the rigor and problem-solving of quantitative methods and STEM is incredibly valuable, especially in healthcare. You are not alone in feeling this pull, and many of the most impactful roles in healthcare today require exactly this kind of interdisciplinary thinking.

Let’s break down your situation and explore paths forward.

What to do about your studies?

Your current major, Health and Societies with a concentration in Healthcare Markets and Finance, is already a fantastic foundation. It inherently blends social science perspectives with the quantitative and business aspects of healthcare. Here’s how you can further optimize it:

  1. Embrace and Connect the “Theoretical” with the “Quantitative”:
    • Reframing Social Sciences: Instead of seeing social sciences as “just theoretical,” view them as providing the frameworks and questions that quantitative analysis can then answer. For example, theories about health disparities (social sciences) can be tested, measured, and understood through statistical analysis of clinical trial data or population health data (quantitative).
    • Look for Quantitative Social Science: Many fields within social sciences are highly quantitative:
      • Economics: Especially health economics, econometrics.
      • Sociology/Demography: Using statistical methods to analyze social trends, health behaviors, and population health.
      • Political Science/Policy Analysis: Quantitative methods to evaluate policy impact.
    • Focus on Research Methods: Seek out courses in advanced research methods, statistics, and program evaluation within the social sciences or public health departments. These courses will teach you how to apply quantitative tools to social questions.
  2. Double Down on Quantitative Skills (without abandoning your core):
    • Minors/Concentrations: Consider a minor or additional concentration in:
      • Statistics: Directly enhances your data analysis skills.
      • Data Science: Covers programming (Python/R), machine learning, data visualization.
      • Computer Science: Even a foundational understanding can be immensely helpful for data manipulation and understanding technical systems.
      • Applied Math/Operations Research: Excellent for optimizing systems, which is crucial in healthcare management.
      • Economics: Especially if you enjoy the market and finance aspect, health economics is a very quantitative field.
    • Targeted Electives: Take more electives in areas like:
      • Econometrics
      • Advanced Statistics (e.g., Regression Analysis, Causal Inference)
      • Programming for Data Analysis (Python, R)
      • Machine Learning
      • Healthcare Analytics/Informatics
    • Project-Based Learning: Seek out opportunities (research assistantships, independent studies) where you can apply quantitative methods to social science questions in healthcare. Your experience with clinical trial data is a perfect example of this.
  3. Leverage Your Wharton Connection:
    • Wharton offers a strong quantitative and business foundation. Ensure you’re taking advantage of courses in finance, operations, and analytics that have a healthcare bent.
    • Your consulting committee experience is valuable – look for projects that allow you to combine strategic thinking with data analysis to solve client problems, perhaps focusing on social impact or health equity challenges within healthcare organizations.

What kind of jobs could you pursue?

This combination of skills and passions positions you perfectly for a variety of high-impact roles that bridge the gap between healthcare, business, social science, and data. You are essentially looking for roles that use quantitative methods to drive social impact and improve health systems.

Here are some career paths, categorized by their primary focus, but all leveraging your unique blend of skills:

I. Data & Analytics Focused (Directly applying STEM/Quant to Healthcare & Social Impact)

  1. Healthcare Data Scientist/Analyst:
    • What you’d do: Analyze large healthcare datasets (clinical, claims, administrative, public health) to identify trends, predict outcomes, optimize resource allocation, and measure the effectiveness of interventions. You could specifically focus on identifying and addressing health disparities.
    • Why it fits: Highly quantitative, direct application to healthcare, and can be used to inform policy and improve care for specific populations, directly addressing social disparities.
    • Where: Hospitals, health systems, insurance companies, pharmaceutical companies, health tech startups, government agencies (CDC, NIH), public health departments, research institutions.
  2. Health Outcomes Researcher:
    • What you’d do: Study the effectiveness of medical treatments, interventions, and health policies on patient outcomes, quality of life, and economic impact. This often involves advanced statistical modeling and real-world data analysis.
    • Why it fits: Quantitative rigor applied to understanding what works best for patients and populations, often with an eye towards equity and value.
    • Where: Pharma/biotech, academic medical centers, contract research organizations (CROs), health plans.
  3. Clinical Informatics Specialist:
    • What you’d do: Bridge the gap between clinical practice and information technology. You’d help design, implement, and optimize electronic health records (EHRs) and other clinical systems to improve patient care, data collection, and decision-making.
    • Why it fits: Combines technical/quantitative skills with an understanding of healthcare operations and patient needs. Can ensure systems are designed with equity and accessibility in mind.
    • Where: Hospitals, health systems, health tech companies.

II. Consulting & Strategy (Leveraging Business Acumen, Quant, and Social Lens)

  1. Healthcare Consultant (Strategy, Operations, Digital Health, Public Health):
    • What you’d do: Advise healthcare organizations (hospitals, pharma, government, non-profits) on strategic challenges, operational efficiency, market entry, digital transformation, or public health initiatives.
    • Why it fits: Your Wharton background, consulting committee experience, and understanding of healthcare markets are highly relevant. You could focus on projects that optimize care delivery, improve access, or address health equity using data-driven insights. Many firms have dedicated public health or social impact arms.
    • Where: Large management consulting firms (McKinsey, Bain, BCG), specialized healthcare consulting firms (Advisory Board, Guidehouse), boutique firms.
  2. Health Policy Analyst/Consultant:
    • What you’d do: Research, analyze, and evaluate healthcare policies and their impact on populations, access, cost, and quality. You’d use data to inform policy recommendations and challenge existing systemic theories.
    • Why it fits: Directly addresses your passion for social sciences, challenging systems, and using quantitative evidence to drive change.
    • Where: Think tanks, government agencies (CMS, HHS), non-profit advocacy groups, consulting firms, academic research centers.

III. Research & Policy (Deep Dive into Social Impact with Quantitative Rigor)

  1. Public Health Researcher/Epidemiologist:
    • What you’d do: Investigate the causes and patterns of disease and health in populations, often with a strong focus on social determinants of health and disparities. This involves robust statistical analysis and research design.
    • Why it fits: Highly quantitative, directly addresses social disparities, and aims to improve population health.
    • Where: Universities, government health agencies (CDC, WHO, state/local health departments), non-profit organizations. (Often requires an MPH or PhD).
  2. Health Economist:
    • What you’d do: Apply economic principles and quantitative methods to study healthcare systems, health behaviors, and the value of health interventions.
    • Why it fits: Combines your interest in markets and finance with rigorous quantitative analysis to understand and improve healthcare efficiency and equity.
    • Where: Academia, government agencies, pharmaceutical companies, health plans, consulting firms.

IV. Industry Roles (Applying your skills within Healthcare Companies)

  1. Market Access Analyst/Manager (Pharma/Biotech):
    • What you’d do: Analyze market dynamics, payer policies, and patient access barriers to ensure innovative treatments reach the patients who need them. This involves understanding health economics, policy, and data.
    • Why it fits: Leverages your Healthcare Markets & Finance background, quantitative skills, and a desire to ensure equitable access to care.
    • Where: Pharmaceutical and biotechnology companies.
  2. Product Manager (Health Tech):
    • What you’d do: Oversee the development of healthcare technology products (e.g., apps for chronic disease management, AI tools for diagnostics). This requires understanding user needs, market dynamics, and technical capabilities, often with a focus on impact.
    • Why it fits: Combines your interest in STEM, healthcare, and potentially designing solutions that address social needs or improve care for underserved populations.
    • Where: Health tech startups, established tech companies with healthcare divisions.

Next Steps & Recommendations:

  1. Talk to People: Conduct informational interviews with professionals in these roles. Ask them about their day-to-day work, the skills they use, and their career paths. Your Wharton network will be invaluable here.
  2. Seek Relevant Internships: Look for internships that allow you to apply your quantitative skills to healthcare problems, ideally with a social impact angle. This could be at a hospital’s analytics department, a public health non-profit, a consulting firm’s healthcare practice, or a health tech startup.
  3. Build Your Portfolio: Continue with data science projects. Showcase your ability to not only run analyses but also to interpret the results in a social context and make actionable recommendations.
  4. Master Key Tools: Ensure proficiency in statistical software (R, Python, SAS, Stata), data visualization tools (Tableau, Power BI), and database querying (SQL).
  5. Consider Graduate School (Eventually): Many of the more specialized roles (e.g., Health Outcomes Researcher, Epidemiologist, Health Policy Analyst, Health Economist) benefit significantly from a Master’s degree (e.g., MPH, MS in Data Science/Biostatistics, MHA, MPP) or even a PhD. However, you can certainly start your career after your undergraduate degree and gain experience first.

You are in a fantastic position to carve out a meaningful and impactful career. Don’t see your interests as conflicting, but rather as complementary strengths that will allow you to approach complex healthcare challenges from a holistic and data-driven perspective. Good luck!
